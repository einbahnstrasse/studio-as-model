Musical Concerns

Transcription

  • especially of “complex sounds”
  • i.e., sounds that are “paradoxical,” “unstable,” neither harmony nor timbre (Tristan Murail)
    • e.g., captured in field recordings
    • e.g., obtained from FM synthesis
  • their mimesis by acoustic instruments
    • a natural extension of transcribing
      jazz + free music as a saxophonist

Psychoacoustics

    • perception research
    • auditory illusion
    • sound morphologies
    • fusion of instrumental aggregates

Orchestration

2 paradigms:
  1. symbolic, syntactical
    • Unlike the highly-encoded and descriptive language used within established historical performance practices, in orchestration, we lack the basic language to describe our most fundamental elements...
    • such as: the genre-defining “symbolic” spectral envelopes used by several historical composers.
    • Naively, we still ascribe the wrong terms to the effects we perceive. For example, “spacing” fails to account for any of an aggregate’s basic timbral properties.
  2. computer-assisted, algorithmic
    • next slide: brief demo of an orchestration “workspace...”

Musical Concerns

More recently, these include...

dynamical systems

  • representations of musical turbulence
  • modeling non-linear ebb + flow
    • in musical time & pitch,
    • in trajectories of spatialized sounds,
    • in swirling, eddying currents between orchestral aggregates and layers,
    • or in the momentary flux of perceptual descriptors
E m e r g e n c e
  • formation of new topologies for emergent phenomena in music: within complex, aggregate sonorities and textures
    • How do we reduce the complexity of continuous and irrationally “fluid” rhythms, for example, to reproduce its idiosyncratic sense of motion in instrumental writing...
    • ...to promote readability among musicians...
    • ...while preserving the integrity of the emergent musical material; its textural and timbral properties, etc.?

“Emergence, unlike the gestalt, refers to a property’s [...] statistical change over time, and therefore lends itself to computational analysis and a dynamic conception of musical form, rather than a traditional representation of musical form as a static and unchanging or monolithic entity...”

—Louis Goldford,

Emergence in the Music of

  • metrical discretizing
    • First of all... WHAT?!
    • How do we reduce the inherent complexity of continuous and irrationally “fluid” rhythms?
    • And package them into sensible meters with useful subdivisions that promote readability?
